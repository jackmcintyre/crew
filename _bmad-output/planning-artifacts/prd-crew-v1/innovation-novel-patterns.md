# Innovation & Novel Patterns

## Detected Innovation Areas

- **A team that forms itself, with persistent personas.** The hiring manager pattern + long-lived persona files per agent is the central novelty. Most agentic tooling either ships a fixed agent (Copilot, Cursor) or a fixed roster of subagents (Devin, OpenHands). The product instead lets the *project* shape the team via an HR-style hiring conversation, and gives every hired agent a persistent file that accretes project knowledge cycle over cycle. The institutional memory a real engineering team builds informally becomes observable and editable. The catalogue-then-generative path (pre-defined roles in v1, agent-drafted roles in Growth) is the trust-staircase analogue of Pattern A → Pattern B.
- **Domain-protected expertise as a team protocol.** Every agent has an explicit `domain:` field; inside it, they insist on judgement; outside it, they yield via a locked handoff phrase. This is the "stay in your lane / defer to the expert" rule, encoded as a protocol rather than a polite suggestion. It neutralises the "AI reviews AI and they agree" rubber-stamping failure mode: a domain-protected security specialist will not let a generalist reviewer wave through an auth miss.
- **"AI engineering team" as a product category, not a feature.** The adjacent market frames AI as a *coding assistant* — Copilot, Cursor, Codeium. The product reframes AI as a *team*: planning, dev, review, retro, plus project-specific specialists, are distinct roles with distinct prompts, permissions, personas, and failure modes. The category is what's novel; the components are individually known.
- **Rigour is the product, code is the byproduct.** Conventional AI coding tooling optimises for code volume or generation speed. This product makes the *engineering scaffolding* — definition of done, standards, retros, calibration — the load-bearing surface. The agents produce code as a side effect of operating against that scaffolding. This inversion is the central design decision.
- **The standards doc as product surface, not agent prompt.** The reviewer's rubric lives in a human-editable, version-controlled file in the target repo, not baked into the agent's prompt. The agent is dumb-ish; the standard is smart-ish. The unit of iteration is the standard, not the prompt. (Inherited from the Pattern A PRD; load-bearing for this product.)
- **Risk-tiered autonomy gated on a measurable agreement metric.** Low-risk auto-merge is permitted only after the verdict-vs-action agreement metric crosses a threshold. The product *measures its own trustworthiness* before granting itself authority. Most "AI ships code autonomously" tooling skips this gate.
- **Continuous flow over sprints for AI-paced work.** Sprints are a human-pacing construct; AI throughput makes them friction. The product commits to continuous flow as the natural shape and makes the filesystem (directory states) the canonical state machine. This is unusual — most "AI agents working concurrently" systems lean on databases, queues, or message brokers, not `mv`.
- **Retros that propose rules, skills, AND team changes.** The retro analyst emits three kinds of artifact: failure-driven rule proposals (what to forbid), success-driven skill proposals (what to codify and reuse), and team-composition proposals (hire a specialist, consider unhiring an unused one). Most AI-improvement loops capture only the first; this product treats positive patterns and team-shape as first-class learning signals — the team self-tunes alongside the standard.
- **The product is designed for its own improvement.** The calibration loop is in scope from day one. A typical v1 ships features and adds learning later; this v1 ships learning *as* the feature, because without it the engineering rigour erodes rather than compounds.

## Market Context & Competitive Landscape

The adjacent market is large and crowded — but the product plays in a different surface than most of it.

- **Coding assistants (Copilot, Cursor, Codeium, Windsurf).** Optimise for "developer types faster." Inside the IDE. The user is an engineer; the bottleneck is keystrokes. This product is in a different surface: the user is not an engineer; the bottleneck is everything around the keystrokes.
- **Agentic dev tools (Devin, OpenHands, SWE-agent, Codex CLI).** Closer territory. They promise autonomous code production. But they're framed as engineering replacements operating in an engineering context — handed tickets, expected to ship. This product is framed as a *team* — handed a vague intent, expected to plan, ship, review, and learn. The scaffolding around the agents is the differentiator.
- **AI PR-review bots (CodeRabbit, Greptile, GitHub's Copilot review).** Framed as "AI helps the human reviewer." This product flips the framing: the human reviewer is the bottleneck for an AI-shipping team, and the review surface is one component of a larger loop that includes planning and retros. The competitive surface is not "best PR review tool"; it is "what unblocks AI-generated PR throughput when the human can't read fast enough."
- **Workflow orchestration tools (LangGraph, AutoGen, CrewAI).** Frameworks for engineers to build agent systems. This product is a *product*, not a framework. The orchestration is opinionated; the user does not assemble it from primitives.

The competitive surface the product plays in is **"AI engineering team for a non-engineer operator"** — a category that does not yet exist as such. The risk is the category doesn't form (the market disagrees that non-engineers want this); the opportunity is being the canonical reference if it does.

## Validation Approach

- **Validate v1 on Jack himself first.** Jack is the canonical user. If the product doesn't work for him by his own standard, it doesn't ship. Dog-fooding the plugin on its own repo is the primary validation loop through Phases 1–5.
- **The agreement metric is the calibration instrument.** Verdict-vs-action agreement ≥80% over two cycles validates the reviewer-and-standard pair. <80% is a falsification signal; either the reviewer or the standard is wrong, and the retro process is designed to make this observable fast.
- **The canonical scenario is the soft-release instrument.** One external reader of Jack's writeup running the product end-to-end and shipping something is the v1 ship-or-don't-ship test. A failed first attempt is high-signal data, not a defeat — it produces the first real friction map for a non-Jack user.
- **Retro convergence is the long-term instrument.** Does the standards doc converge (criteria added and removed at a stable rate) or oscillate (large churn each cycle)? Convergence signals the calibration loop is finding equilibrium; oscillation signals the standard is chasing noise. This validates whether the *learning* part of the product is working, separately from the *shipping* part.

## Risk Mitigation (for the innovation thesis)

- **"AI reviews AI" rubber-stamping.** Same model family producing both dev code and reviewer verdict can produce a green-on-green failure mode. Mitigation: the *team protocol* itself is the strongest mitigation — domain-protected specialists with their own personas (e.g. a security specialist that yields *to* on auth changes) refuse to rubber-stamp inside their domain. Plus: reviewer prompt is built around the *external* standards doc, not introspection; the standard is human-authored; rubber-stamping shows up in the agreement metric (reviewer always green, user often disagrees) and the retro proposes standard sharpening or a team change.
- **Hiring manager mis-hires.** A hiring manager that always proposes the default roster is just a renamed configurator; one that proposes a wrong specialist sends the team off shape. Mitigation: in v1 the hiring manager is catalogue-bound (it cannot invent roles), defaults to the general-purpose roster, and must justify any specialist addition with a sentence the user can challenge. The retro analyst tracks predicted-vs-actual impact of hires across cycles; bad hires surface as retro signals and trigger unhire proposals.
- **Persona drift / poisoning.** Long-lived persona files accrete content cycle over cycle. A bad cycle — wrong lessons recorded, hallucinated facts persisted — could pollute future behaviour. Mitigation: persona files are *plain Markdown*, human-readable, editable in any text editor; the user can read what their agents "know" and prune it. Persona updates are diff-then-confirm in v1, not silent appends; the user sees what the agent wants to record before it sticks.
- **Innovation collapsing into "another agent framework."** If the product devolves into "build your own pipeline with these primitives," it loses the leverage of being a *product*. Mitigation: the canonical scenario is the unit of testing; if a v1 ship requires the user to assemble primitives, v1 has failed the test, regardless of what the product can technically do.
- **Innovation outrunning trust.** Auto-merging medium- or high-risk PRs before the agreement metric earns it would burn trust faster than the calibration loop rebuilds it. Mitigation: the agreement-metric gate is encoded in scope (medium/high *never* auto-merge in v1; low-risk only after the threshold).
- **Innovation collapsing into "definition of perfect."** Standards doc bloat is the canonical innovation failure mode for products like this. Mitigation: hard cap of 10 criteria in v1; growth only from observed misses; explicit "remove or relax" target per cycle. Inherited verbatim from the Pattern A PRD.
- **Innovation theatre via retros.** Retros that produce proposals nobody accepts are decoration, not learning. Mitigation: accepted-proposal rate per cycle is itself a metric (≥1 per cycle); a zero rate triggers a retrospective-on-the-retrospective, not silent continuation.
